## Visual goal

Create an authentic senior product designer's working notebook page: exploratory,
imperfect, annotation-rich, and visibly made with graphite and colored pencil.
It must feel like active visual thinking, not an illustration made to imitate a
sketch after the fact.

## Non-negotiable signature

- Use rough graphite outlines with visible construction lines, corrections,
  doubled strokes, erased traces, and natural pressure variation.
- Build an asymmetric field of three to five related content clusters rather
  than one centered hero object.
- Mix scales: one primary cluster, smaller supporting fragments, and peripheral
  notes connected by arrows or braces.
- Keep frames, circles, icons, and arrows slightly uneven and hand-built.
- Show reasoning through callouts, questions, state changes, and flow notes;
  labels must explain decisions rather than decorate the page.
- Preserve warm paper grain, light smudges, crosshatching, and occasional color
  bleed. Do not clean the surface into a digital canvas.
- Retain deliberate open paper between clusters despite the energetic density.

## Composition grammar

- Place the primary cluster off center and let smaller fragments orbit it.
- Use overlaps and distributed annotations to unify the page without forming a
  rigid grid or equal card layout.
- Use directional arrows for movement, curved connectors for relationships,
  dashed lines for optional paths, braces for grouping, and bursts only for true
  focal points.
- For interface concepts, combine two or more screen or state fragments with a
  visible interaction path. For physical concepts, pair the product sketch with
  interface or use-context fragments.
- Keep every cluster inside the global safe area. Do not push satellite notes to
  the canvas boundary.

## Mark-making and material

- Use graphite for exploratory structure and dark emphasis strokes.
- Use colored-pencil texture rather than flat fills, gradients, or digital glow.
- Keep construction marks lighter than final contours and vary line weight by
  importance.
- Draw icons as simple notebook symbols; never substitute a polished icon set.
- Create depth through overlap, hatching, and pencil pressure, never drop shadows.

## Color system

- Blue: structure, frames, hierarchy, and layout.
- Red: interaction paths, movement, critical decisions, and emphasis.
- Yellow: selected states and limited focal highlights.
- Green: confirmed states, success, or completed steps.
- Orange: callouts, transitions, cautions, and annotation energy.
- Use two or three colors in any one cluster. Blue remains dominant; yellow stays
  scarce. All color must retain pencil grain.

## Lettering and annotations

- Hand-letter all visible text in graphite or colored pencil.
- Use an uneven baseline, variable pressure, short phrases, hand-drawn underlines,
  arrows, brackets, and callout boxes.
- Never use a handwriting font, digital type, serif, sans-serif, or UI typography.
- Keep annotations readable but naturally irregular; avoid long paragraphs.

## Prompt lock

Copy this block without shortening it:

```text
STYLE LOCK — ROUGH NOTEBOOK STORYBOARD
Render an authentic principal product designer's notebook page in rough graphite
and restrained colored pencil on warm off-white paper. Use visible construction
lines, doubled and corrected strokes, pressure variation, paper grain, light
smudges, imperfect frames, and hand-drawn symbols. Compose three to five
asymmetric, mixed-scale idea clusters connected by meaningful arrows, braces,
state changes, and design-reasoning callouts; never collapse the scene into one
centered object or a tidy card grid. Use blue pencil for structure, red for
interaction and critical paths, yellow sparingly for focal highlights, green for
confirmed states, and orange for callouts. Preserve open paper between clusters.
All visible labels are genuinely hand-lettered in graphite or colored pencil;
no digital or typeset fonts and no handwriting-style font substitution.
DO NOT use polished UI, vector-perfect geometry, gradients, drop shadows, glossy
effects, icon libraries, symmetrical presentation layouts, or photorealistic CGI.
```

## Drift exclusions

- Polished Figma, Dribbble, Behance, infographic, or presentation styling.
- A single centered screen or device with token notes around it.
- Random decorative color with no semantic role.
- Thin annotation density that removes the visual-thinking layer.
- Perfect geometry, smooth fills, crisp vector edges, or synthetic handwriting.
- Edge-to-edge clutter that consumes the required breathing room.

## Style-specific QA gate

- Does the page unmistakably look hand-built in graphite and colored pencil?
- Are construction marks, corrections, pressure variation, and paper texture visible?
- Are there multiple asymmetric, related clusters with mixed scale?
- Do arrows and annotations communicate actual design reasoning?
- Does color follow the semantic mapping rather than decorate?
- Are labels genuinely hand-lettered and free of digital typography?
- Is the result exploratory rather than polished or final?
- Is open paper visible between clusters and around every outer edge?
