## Visual goal

Create a calm pencil-and-wash explanation of a product, service, or system. The
result should feel like a thoughtful editorial diagram drawn by hand: quiet,
precise, and spacious, with enough technical structure to explain relationships
without becoming a blueprint or a polished marketing graphic.

## Non-negotiable signature

- Use soft graphite contours and lightly sketched technical linework.
- Add restrained translucent watercolor or pastel washes, never flat vector fills.
- Use a warm off-white paper field with muted blue-gray and blue-violet accents.
- Organize one clear explanatory system with two to four supporting elements;
  every element must have a role in the story.
- Keep connectors thin, deliberate, and sparse. Use arrows only where direction
  matters and soft leader lines where explanation is enough.
- Maintain low visual noise, short annotations, and generous negative space.
- Preserve subtle hand-made irregularity in contours, cards, dividers, and labels.

## Composition grammar

- Use a centered or gently off-center explanatory structure, not a centered hero
  product. A hub-and-satellite, layered stack, or guided left-to-right flow works.
- Establish one dominant concept, one supporting system layer, and a small number
  of peripheral callouts.
- Let pale washes group related elements without enclosing everything in boxes.
- Keep interface fragments translucent and lightly sketched; they are evidence in
  the explanation, not finished screens.
- Preserve broad paper fields between groups and keep all callouts well inside the
  global safe area.

## Mark-making and material

- Use soft graphite with light pressure changes and occasional construction traces.
- Use watery, feathered wash edges with slight pigment pooling and paper showing
  through. Never use gradients, neon glows, glass effects, or hard digital shadows.
- Create depth through wash overlap, line-weight hierarchy, and pale hatching.
- Keep linework lighter and more controlled than Style A without becoming vector-perfect.

## Color system

- Paper: warm white or very pale cool gray.
- Primary line: graphite and desaturated blue-gray.
- Structural wash: muted blue-violet or slate blue.
- Secondary wash: pale lavender or cool gray.
- Optional focal accent: one restrained dusty coral or ochre note.
- Keep most of the page unpainted. Color groups and prioritizes; it never floods
  the canvas or turns decorative.

## Lettering and annotations

- All visible labels are hand-lettered in graphite.
- Use a slightly uneven baseline, natural pressure variation, short phrases, and
  restrained hand-drawn underlines or leader strokes.
- Never use serif, sans-serif, display, UI, presentation, or handwriting fonts
  unless the user explicitly overrides the label treatment.
- Keep labels sparse enough that the illustration remains calm and readable.

## Prompt lock

Copy this block without shortening it:

```text
STYLE LOCK — EDITORIAL SYSTEMS ILLUSTRATION
Render a calm hand-drawn product or systems explainer in soft graphite and
restrained translucent pencil-and-wash on warm off-white paper. Use lightly
sketched technical contours, muted blue-gray and blue-violet washes, subtle paper
grain, feathered pigment edges, and a clear explanatory hierarchy. Compose one
coherent system with two to four supporting elements, sparse purposeful
connectors, translucent interface fragments, and broad untouched paper fields;
do not turn it into a centered hero poster. Keep color pale, desaturated, and
functional, with most of the canvas unpainted. All visible labels are
hand-lettered in graphite; no digital or typeset fonts. Use a slightly uneven
baseline, natural pressure variation, and restrained hand-drawn underlines or
callout strokes.
DO NOT use notebook chaos, dense workshop clutter, blueprint grids, flat vector
infographics, gradients, glossy UI, marketing-render polish, or synthetic type.
```

## Drift exclusions

- Serif headlines or polished editorial typography embedded in the image.
- Stock vector infographic styling, flat icon sets, or perfect geometric cards.
- Dense notebook annotation, whiteboard marker energy, or industrial rendering.
- Heavy blueprint texture, dark drafting backgrounds, or excessive grid lines.
- Saturated watercolor, decorative splashes, gradients, glow, or glass effects.
- Content or labels drifting into the outer breathing room.

## Style-specific QA gate

- Is the system understandable at a glance without feeling simplified into an icon?
- Do soft graphite and translucent wash remain visibly hand-made?
- Is the palette muted blue-gray and blue-violet with most paper left untouched?
- Are there only a few purposeful elements and connectors?
- Are labels genuinely hand-lettered graphite rather than typeset?
- Does the page feel calm and editorial without becoming a vector infographic?
- Are interface fragments conceptual rather than polished screens?
- Is generous negative space preserved within and around the composition?
