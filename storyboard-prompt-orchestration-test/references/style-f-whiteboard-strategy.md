## Visual goal

Create a legible strategy map that feels captured from a strong live workshop.
It should show how concepts, actors, stages, loops, and decisions relate through
bold marker groupings and a clear reading path, while retaining the spontaneity
and minor irregularity of a real dry-erase board.

## Non-negotiable signature

- Use a matte white dry-erase surface with faint erasure ghosts and subtle marker texture.
- Draw with bold dark charcoal or black marker plus restrained blue, red, and green accents.
- Organize four to seven concept groups using hand-drawn containers, swimlanes,
  loops, stages, or relationship clusters.
- Establish one dominant reading path and distinguish supporting feedback loops.
- Use chunkier linework, rounded hand-lettering, uneven boxes, and visible marker overlap.
- Make grouping and relationships more important than illustration or interface detail.
- Preserve open whiteboard between groups so the logic scans from a distance.

## Composition grammar

- Choose one governing layout: left-to-right stages, top-to-bottom service flow,
  concentric system map, swimlanes, or center-and-feedback loops.
- Use large group labels, medium concept labels, and small action notes.
- Connect groups with bold directional arrows; use thinner lines for secondary
  relationships and dashed lines for assumptions or unresolved links.
- Use simple stick figures, circles, boxes, clouds, and hand-drawn symbols only
  when they clarify actors or system roles.
- Use pale sticky-note rectangles sparingly for decisions, questions, or unresolved items.
- Keep all group containers, arrows, and labels inside the global safe area and
  leave visible gutters between adjacent groups.

## Mark-making and material

- Use rounded dry-erase marker strokes with natural thickness changes, overlap,
  occasional squeak gaps, and slightly feathered ends.
- Keep boxes and circles visibly hand-drawn rather than geometrically perfect.
- Allow faint erased traces behind the current map, but never enough to reduce legibility.
- Use double underlines, brackets, loops, and arrowheads as facilitation marks.
- Avoid pencil delicacy, watercolor wash, technical drafting grids, shadows, and 3D effects.

## Color system

- Black or charcoal: primary structure, labels, and group boundaries.
- Blue: system relationships, neutral flows, and shared services.
- Red: tensions, blockers, risks, or critical transitions.
- Green: outcomes, decisions, opportunities, or confirmed paths.
- Pale yellow: optional sticky-note questions or choices.
- Use color semantically and sparingly; do not give every group its own decorative color.

## Lettering and annotations

- Use bold hand-lettered marker text with natural stroke and baseline variation.
- Keep labels short, large enough to scan, and organized into three size levels.
- Use verbs on arrows, nouns in nodes, and questions inside decision or uncertainty notes.
- Never use digital or typeset fonts, handwriting-style fonts, tiny UI labels, or paragraphs.
- Use underlines and boxes for hierarchy without turning every word into a badge.

```text
DO NOT use delicate editorial drawing, blueprint grids, industrial realism,
polished process-infographic cards, tiny interface details, photoreal scenes,
decorative rainbow color, shadows, gradients, or over-rendering.
```

## Drift exclusions

- A neat vector process infographic with perfect cards and uniform connectors.
- Random sticky-note collage with no governing flow or group hierarchy.
- Dense wall-to-wall annotation that cannot be scanned from a distance.
- Tiny UI screens, industrial product sketches, editorial watercolor, or blueprint texture.
- Decorative rainbow marker use without semantic meaning.
- Digital fonts, synthetic handwriting, perfect geometry, shadows, or 3D effects.

## Style-specific QA gate

- Is one governing map structure and reading path immediately clear?
- Are there four to seven meaningful groups with visible separation?
- Do primary arrows, secondary relationships, and assumptions look distinct?
- Does marker texture feel real without becoming messy or illegible?
- Is color sparse and semantically consistent?
- Are labels large, hand-lettered, and organized into three scales?
- Does the map feel facilitation-led rather than infographic-polished?
- Is whiteboard visibly open between groups and around every outer edge?
