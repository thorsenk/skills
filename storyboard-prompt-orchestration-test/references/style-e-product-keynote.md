## Visual goal

Create an executive-facing concept board that is composed, premium, and easy to
present while remaining unmistakably exploratory. It should communicate one
strategic product idea through a dominant concept fragment, a few supporting
views, and restrained diagram overlays rather than through marketing spectacle.

## Non-negotiable signature

- Use a warm near-white field with charcoal, muted indigo, and one restrained
  copper or amber accent.
- Establish one dominant concept fragment supported by two or three smaller
  fragments, not a collage of equal cards.
- Combine refined graphite concept drawing with restrained translucent color
  fields and thin diagrammatic overlays.
- Preserve visible concept traces: construction lines, loose edges, alternate
  states, material swatches, or brief process annotations.
- Use strong hierarchy, generous negative space, deliberate alignment, and a
  calm presentation rhythm.
- Keep interface or product fragments conceptual and selective; do not render
  complete production-ready screens or photoreal products.
- Limit text to a compact title, short labels, and a few strategic callouts.

## Composition grammar

- Place the dominant concept off center and let it occupy roughly half of the
  usable content area.
- Arrange supporting views along one alignment axis or a restrained diagonal.
- Use one thin explanatory path, sequence, or relationship overlay rather than a
  dense network of arrows.
- Use scale and open space for hierarchy before adding boxes, borders, or color.
- Keep supporting fragments unboxed when possible; if a container is necessary,
  use a fine line or pale tint rather than a card shadow.
- Preserve the global safe area and leave a visibly quiet region that could hold
  a speaker's narrative rather than more image content.

## Mark-making and material

- Use controlled graphite linework with a few visible search strokes and edge variations.
- Use translucent matte color fields, light pencil shading, and restrained hatch marks.
- Keep overlays thin and exact enough for presentation but not vector-perfect.
- Suggest depth through overlap and value contrast, never cinematic lighting,
  glassmorphism, bevels, or glossy 3D rendering.
- Use simple hand-drawn diagram symbols rather than stock icons.

## Color system

- Field: warm white, ivory, or very pale stone.
- Primary drawing: charcoal and soft graphite.
- Structural accent: muted indigo or deep slate blue.
- Focal accent: one restrained copper, amber, or terracotta.
- Optional secondary neutral: pale cool gray or sand.
- Keep at least two-thirds of the field visually light and avoid gradients,
  multicolor feature coding, and decorative color washes.

## Lettering and annotations

- Default to one restrained typeset sans-serif hierarchy for the compact title,
  short labels, and small strategic annotations.
- Use no more than three text scales and one type family; keep typography
  subordinate to the concept imagery.
- Place exact copy in an editable overlay when fidelity matters.
- Do not imitate handwriting, mix type styles, use dramatic display lettering,
  or fill the image with presentation copy.
- If the user overrides the style to hand-lettered text, state and repeat that
  override explicitly in every prompt.

## Prompt lock

Copy this block without shortening it:

```text
STYLE LOCK — PRODUCT KEYNOTE CONCEPT BOARD
Render an executive-facing but still exploratory product concept board on a warm
near-white field. Use refined graphite concept drawing, visible construction
traces, restrained translucent matte color fields, charcoal structure, muted
indigo, and one sparse copper or amber accent. Establish one off-center dominant
concept fragment supported by only two or three smaller aligned views and one
thin explanatory overlay. Use scale, alignment, and broad negative space for
hierarchy; keep a quiet region free of content. Show alternate states, process
marks, material hints, or brief annotations so the work cannot be mistaken for
a final product comp. Use one compact typeset sans-serif hierarchy with no more
than three scales, subordinate to the imagery.
DO NOT use glossy dashboard mockups, photoreal product renders, Behance-style
presentation polish, stock gradients, glass panels, cinematic lighting, giant
headlines, card grids, excessive copy, or launch-ad spectacle.
```

## Drift exclusions

- A glossy keynote launch slide, advertisement, or finished dashboard comp.
- One photoreal hero object with dramatic lighting and no process evidence.
- Equal card grids, oversized headlines, dense bullet copy, or decorative icon rows.
- Multiple font families, dramatic display type, fake handwriting, or text-led layout.
- Gradients, glass effects, neon glow, lens flare, or generic premium-tech styling.
- Over-cleaned geometry that removes every construction trace and alternate state.

## Style-specific QA gate

- Is there one clearly dominant strategic concept with only a few supporting views?
- Does the result feel presentation-ready without pretending to be final design?
- Are construction traces or alternate states still visibly present?
- Is the palette limited to warm light field, charcoal, indigo, and one warm accent?
- Is typography compact, consistent, and subordinate to the imagery?
- Are glossy marketing effects and photoreal polish absent?
- Does a quiet narrative region remain visibly open?
- Are all fragments and labels contained within the outer safe area?
